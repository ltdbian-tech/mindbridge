Someone you love
is hurting.

You don't need to be a therapist to save a life. You just need to show up, ask directly, and stay. This page will walk you through exactly what to do — step by step.

Warning Signs to Watch For

Trust your gut. If something feels off, it probably is.

Behavioral Changes

  • Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities they used to enjoy
  • Sleeping much more or much less than usual
  • Acting recklessly, aggressively, or out of character
  • Giving away prized possessions or saying goodbye in unusual ways
  • Searching online for ways to die or buying means of self-harm

What They Might Say

  • "I feel like a burden to everyone."
  • "Nothing will ever get better."
  • "Everyone would be better off without me."
  • "I can't do this anymore."
  • "I'm just so tired of everything."
  • Talking about death or dying more than usual

Emotional & Physical Signs

  • Sudden calmness after a period of depression (may mean they've made a decision)
  • Extreme mood swings
  • Unexplained aches, pains, or fatigue
  • Increased substance use

What to Say

You won't make things worse by asking. Research shows asking directly lowers risk.

Script: How to ask directly

"I've noticed you've seemed really down lately, and I'm worried about you. Have you been thinking about hurting yourself or suicide?"

Why this works: It shows you care, you've noticed, and you're not afraid of the answer. Direct questions get direct answers. Euphemisms don't.

If they say YES

"Thank you for trusting me. I want to help you stay safe. Can we call a crisis line together right now? I'll stay with you."

Do not: promise secrecy, leave them alone, debate whether their pain is valid, or try to fix everything yourself. Do: stay, listen, remove means if possible, and get professional help involved.

If they say NO

"Okay. I'm still here for you, and I want you to know you can tell me anything — even the hard stuff. Can we check in again tomorrow?"

Even if they deny it, you've opened the door. Keep showing up. Follow up. Your consistency matters more than one perfect conversation.

What NOT to Say

These are common but harmful. Avoid them.

"You have so much to live for." — In their mind, they don't. This feels dismissive.

"Other people have it worse." — Pain isn't a competition. This invalidates their experience.

"Just think positive." — If they could, they would. This implies they're choosing their pain.

"You're being selfish." — They already feel like a burden. This confirms it.

"I know how you feel." — Unless you've truly been there, this can feel minimizing. Focus on them, not you.

The 3-Step Action Plan

1

Ask directly. Use the script above. Stay calm. Listen more than you talk.

2

Don't leave them alone. If they're in immediate danger, call emergency services. Remove means if you can do so safely.

3

Get professional help involved. Call a crisis line together. Go to the ER if needed. You are not abandoning them — you are helping them survive.
