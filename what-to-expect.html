What happens when
you reach out?

The #1 reason people don't call for help is fear of the unknown. Will they judge me? Will they force me into a hospital? Will I get in trouble? Here's the truth — from people who've actually made these calls.

Common Fears, Debunked

"They'll think I'm overreacting."

Crisis counselors hear everything from "I'm having a bad day" to "I'm about to act on my plan." There is no threshold for "bad enough." If you're struggling, you deserve support. Period.

"They'll send the police to my house."

For text and chat lines (Crisis Text Line, etc.), this almost never happens unless you explicitly say you are in immediate danger and can't stay safe. For 988, counselors prioritize keeping you safe voluntarily. Police involvement is a last resort, not a first response.

"I'll get locked up against my will."

Involuntary hospitalization is rare and only happens when there is an immediate risk you can't contract for safety. Most of the time, crisis lines help you make a safety plan and connect you to outpatient care. You have more control than you think.

"My parents / boss / partner will find out."

Crisis lines are confidential. For adults, they don't contact your family or employer. For minors, mandatory reporting varies by state, but the counselor will tell you upfront if they need to report. You can ask before sharing anything.

"I don't know what to say when they answer."

You can literally say "I don't know what to say." The counselor will guide you. You don't need to have a script. You don't need to explain everything. Just showing up on the call is enough.

Step by Step: What Actually Happens

📞 When you call 988 (US)

  1. An automated voice asks you to press 1 for Veterans, 2 for Spanish, or stay on the line.
  2. A real human answers — usually within 30 seconds. They'll say something like "Crisis Line, my name is Alex."
  3. They'll ask your name (you can use a fake one) and what's going on.
  4. They'll listen. They won't rush you. They won't hang up on you.
  5. They may ask if you have a plan, means, or timeline. This is to assess risk — not to judge you.
  6. Together, you'll make a plan to stay safe. They might offer to call you back.
  7. The call ends when you feel ready. Average call: 15-25 minutes.

"I was terrified the first time I called 988. The counselor just... listened. For 20 minutes. No one had listened to me like that in years." — A survivor

💬 When you text a crisis line

  1. Text your keyword (e.g., "HOME" to 741741) or start a chat on the website.
  2. An automated bot responds first: "Hi, we're connecting you to a counselor."
  3. Within 5 minutes, a real person joins the chat.
  4. They type slower than a friend — because they're reading carefully and choosing words with care.
  5. You can take breaks. You can say "I need a minute" and come back.
  6. When the conversation ends, they may send follow-up resources.

"Texting felt safer than talking. I could cry and type at the same time without anyone hearing my voice shake." — A survivor

🏥 When you go to the ER

  1. Tell the triage nurse: "I'm having thoughts of hurting myself and I don't feel safe."
  2. You'll be seen quickly — suicidal ideation is treated as an emergency.
  3. A nurse will ask you to change into a gown (for safety screening).
  4. A mental health professional will talk with you about what's happening.
  5. They'll ask about your plan, your support system, and what has helped before.
  6. Most people are NOT admitted. If you can make a safety plan and agree to follow-up, you go home with resources.
  7. If you are admitted, it's usually 24-72 hours for stabilization. It's not punishment — it's protection.

"The ER nurse held my hand while I cried. She said 'You're doing the right thing by coming here.' I believed her." — A survivor

🛋️ Your first therapy appointment

  1. You'll fill out forms about your history. Be as honest as you can — this helps them help you.
  2. The therapist will ask why you're there. You can say "I don't know where to start" and they'll guide you.
  3. They may ask about trauma, family history, and current symptoms. This is standard, not invasive.
  4. They won't fix you in one session. The first appointment is mostly about understanding you.
  5. You can ask them anything: their approach, what to expect, how long therapy usually takes.
  6. If you don't click with them, it's okay to try someone else. The right fit matters.

"My therapist didn't fix me. But she taught me that feeling suicidal wasn't my fault — it was a signal that my needs weren't being met. That changed everything." — A survivor

The bottom line

Reaching out is scary. But the people on the other end of these lines? They chose this work because they care. They want you to call. They are waiting for you. You won't be a burden — you'll be exactly why they show up.
